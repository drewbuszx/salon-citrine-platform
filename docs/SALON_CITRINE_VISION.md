# Salon Citrine — Product Vision

**From the builder who watched a generation of salon software get it wrong.**

---

## The thesis

Salon professionals don't fail because they lack software. They fail because the software they have was built for investors, not for the chair.

**Boulevard** sells enterprise complexity at enterprise prices. Twelve-month contracts. Demo-only trials. A contact center, payroll module, and marketing suite you'll never fully configure — while the front desk still plays phone tag over a cancellation at 4:47 PM on a Saturday. It's powerful. It's also heavy, expensive, and designed to look impressive in a sales deck more than it is to feel inevitable at 7 AM when you're opening the salon alone.

**GlossGenius** got the aesthetic right. Clean, modern, Instagram-native. But beneath the polish: shallow operational depth. No real waitlist intelligence. No formula vault. No retail brain at checkout. Pretty booking, thin salon.

**Vagaro** tried to be everything for everyone. Marketplace, fitness, pets, payroll, 50 integrations. The result is clutter — menus within menus, features you'll never find, and a product that feels like it was assembled, not composed.

**Salon Citrine wins on three things nobody else combines:**

1. **Operational intelligence, not operational noise.** Every screen answers a question a professional actually asks: *Who's waiting? What's low? Where's the gap? What did we do last time?* Not 47 report types. Not a settings page that requires a certification.

2. **Stylist sovereignty.** Each professional owns their book, their link, their portfolio, their availability rules — without waiting on an admin to change a checkbox. The salon is a collective of micro-brands, not a monolith with staff rows in a spreadsheet.

3. **Guest experience that feels like Apple, not enterprise SaaS.** Passwordless return. Embed book on the salon site. One tap to rebook. No account required to start. The client never feels like they're logging into payroll software to get highlights.

We are not building Boulevard with a different logo. We are building the salon operating system that should have existed when I retired — and didn't, because everyone chased the same feature checklist instead of asking what actually makes a professional's day better.

## Ten ideas that would impress a generation

### 1. Intelligent day board

Not empty cells on a grid. A day board that *thinks*.

When a cancellation opens at 2:30 PM, the board doesn't just show a hole — it surfaces ranked gap-fill suggestions: clients whose rebook window is due, waitlist matches within preference score, and processing-time opportunities where a blowout can nest inside a color service. The front desk sees *"3 good fits for this slot"* with one-tap book, not a blank square and a phone tree.

Configurable ideal gaps (30/60/90 min). Stylist-specific ranking. "Best next" slots highlighted, not just chronologically listed.

### 2. Client memory

Every visit builds a living profile — not a static CRM card.

Formula history with photo proof. Allergy and sensitivity flags surfaced at booking *and* checkout. Preferred beverage, conversation notes, last retail purchase, rebook cadence learned from behavior. When Sarah sits in the chair, the stylist sees *what happened last time* without opening three tabs. When she books online, the system remembers her preferred pro and typical service stack.

Memory compounds. The tenth visit should feel effortless. Today, most software resets the relationship every time.

### 3. One-tap rebook loop

Checkout is not an ending. It's the beginning of the next appointment.

At close-out: "Book Sarah again in 4, 6, or 8 weeks?" One tap. Stylist preference learned over time — if she always rebooks at 6 weeks with Jamie, that's the default. Confirmation sent before she leaves the chair. No "we'll call you to schedule" lies.

The rebook loop is the highest-ROI feature in salon software. Almost nobody does it well because it requires checkout + calendar + client memory to work as one motion.

### 4. Waitlist that actually books

Waitlist is not a spreadsheet of hopeful names.

When a slot opens, auto-match against active waitlist entries by preference score: preferred stylist, service, date window, time-of-day flexibility, client lifetime value, how long they've waited. Notify the top match with a time-limited hold. If they decline, cascade to the next. Front desk sees *"Matched: Elena for 3 PM color with Jess"* — confirm or override.

Boulevard makes clients join a waitlist and then makes staff manually hunt. That's not a waitlist. That's a todo list with extra steps.

### 5. Stylist-owned micro-brand

Each professional is a business within the business.

Personal book link (`book.salon.com/jamie`). Portfolio gallery. Custom availability rules (no Tuesdays, processing blocks, online-bookable vs. request-only services). Direct rebook from their clients. Revenue attribution without a commission spreadsheet nightmare.

Admin sets guardrails. Stylists run their books. No bottleneck every time someone wants to block lunch on a Thursday.

### 6. Retail intelligence

Checkout asks: *"Clients like Sarah bought Olaplex No. 3 after a balayage."*

Not generic upsell prompts. Recommendations tied to service just performed, client purchase history, and live inventory. If we're out of stock, don't suggest it. If she bought it last visit, suggest the companion product. Retail becomes consultative, not pushy.

Salon retail margin is real money. Software that ignores it at checkout is leaving 20–30% of revenue on the table.

### 7. Silent ops

Reminders, confirmations, intake forms, deposit collection — zero front-desk phone tag.

Automated SMS/email at configurable intervals. Card on file for no-show protection. Digital intake before arrival so the stylist reads preferences, not discovers them mid-consult. Deposits collected at book, not argued at the desk.

The best front desk experience is one where the phone doesn't ring for things software should handle.

### 8. Formula vault

Color and chemical notes with photo proof. Searchable. Tied to client and visit date.

"Show me Sarah's last balayage formula." One search. Formula, developer ratio, processing time, brand, and a photo of the result. Stylists stop digging through paper cards, Notes apps, and Instagram DMs.

This is the feature stylists will tattoo on their laptops. Nobody outside the industry understands how much time is lost to formula archaeology.

### 9. Team pulse dashboard

Today's revenue pace vs. typical Tuesday. Chair utilization by stylist. Waitlist heat map. Low stock alerts. Checked-in vs. late vs. waiting.

One glance. Not a report builder. Not twelve clicks into Analytics. The owner opens the app at 11 AM and knows: *We're pacing 12% ahead. Jess is fully booked. Three people on the waitlist want afternoon color. Shampoo is low.*

This is the heartbeat of the salon. We ship the pulse strip first; the full dashboard follows.

### 10. Guest experience — embed book, passwordless return

Booking widget on the salon website that matches the brand — not an iframe that looks like a different company. Passwordless return for existing clients (magic link or SMS code). Service customization that updates price and duration live. Self-reschedule within policy. Waitlist join from the flow.

The client should feel like they're booking with the salon they love, not logging into a vendor portal. Apple-simple. No account required to start. Remembered when they return.

## What we ship in phases (90 days)

### Phase 1 — Front desk can't live without it (Days 1–30)

**Goal:** Replace the phone tag. Make the day board the source of truth.

| Ship | Why |
|------|-----|
| Waitlist auto-match (v1) | Cancellations become bookings, not panic |
| Check-in board (waiting / arrived / late / out) | Front desk sees the room, not a list |
| Sidebar fix + list page polish | Professional chrome everywhere, not just Manage |
| Embed book widget | Salon site → book flow, branded |
| Team Pulse strip | One-glance heartbeat on dashboard |

**Exit criteria:** Front desk runs a full Saturday without calling a client to confirm something software should have handled.

### Phase 2 — Stylist love (Days 31–60)

**Goal:** Make every professional feel the software knows their craft.

| Ship | Why |
|------|-----|
| Formula vault (v1) | Searchable color/chemical notes + photo |
| One-tap rebook loop | Checkout → next appointment in one motion |
| Client memory panel | Last visit, formula, preferences at chair-side |
| Intelligent gap-fill suggestions | Day board ranks best fits, not just empty slots |

**Exit criteria:** A stylist says "I can't go back to Boulevard for color clients."

### Phase 3 — Owner love (Days 61–90)

**Goal:** Revenue visibility without a CFO.

| Ship | Why |
|------|-----|
| Team pulse dashboard (full) | Revenue pace, utilization, waitlist heat |
| Retail intelligence at checkout | Service-aware product suggestions |
| Commission-ready reports | Stylist attribution without spreadsheet export hell |
| Silent ops hardening | Reminders, intake, deposits — production-grade |

**Exit criteria:** Owner opens app once a day and trusts the number.

## What we deliberately DON'T build

Focus is a feature. These are traps:

| Don't build | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| **Payroll** | ADP and Gusto exist. Salons don't switch software for paychecks. |
| **Client marketplace / discovery** | We're not trying to be GlossGenius's Instagram. Our clients belong to the salon, not a platform. |
| **50 integrations** | Stripe, email/SMS, calendar sync, maybe QuickBooks export. That's the list. Every integration is a maintenance contract. |
| **Contact center / VoIP** | Front desk needs silent ops, not another phone system. |
| **Membership engine (v1)** | Packages and gift cards first. Recurring billing is Phase 4 at earliest. |
| **Multi-location enterprise** | One salon, done perfectly. Expansion comes after product-market fit, not before. |
| **Fitness / pets / spa generic** | Salon Citrine is for salons. Vertical focus beats horizontal mediocrity. |
